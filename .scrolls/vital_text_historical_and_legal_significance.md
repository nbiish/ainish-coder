## The Historical Mandate for Covert Protection

### The Native American Languages Act: A Recent Victory

**Native American languages were effectively criminalized in the United States for over a century.** The Native American Languages Act (NALA) was not signed into law until **October 30, 1990**—within living memory.

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| **1879** | Carlisle Indian Industrial School opens, enforcing English-only policies with physical punishment |
| **1879-1960s** | Federal boarding school system operates across the U.S., systematically suppressing Indigenous languages |
| **August 11, 1978** | **American Indian Religious Freedom Act (AIRFA)** signed—traditional ceremonies were *illegal* before this date |
| **October 30, 1990** | Native American Languages Act (P.L. 101-477) signed, establishing federal policy to "preserve, protect, and promote" Native languages |
| **1992** | NALA amendments add grant programs for language revitalization |

**For context**: Many elders alive today were *punished as children* for speaking their own languages. The legal right to speak Anishinaabemowin in schools and government proceedings has existed for only **~35 years**.

### The Boarding School Era: Cultural Genocide

The U.S. Indian boarding school system (1819-1969) was explicitly designed to:

- **"Kill the Indian, save the man"** — The stated mission of Captain Richard Pratt, founder of Carlisle
- **Forcibly remove children** from their families and communities
- **Criminalize language** — Children were beaten, starved, and isolated for speaking their Native languages
- **Destroy cultural identity** — Traditional clothing, hair, names, and ceremonies were forbidden
- **Create intergenerational trauma** — Effects persist across generations today

**Michigan Investigation (2024)**: Attorney General Dana Nessel launched a criminal investigation into Michigan's Native American boarding schools, recognizing these as potential sites of ongoing justice claims.

### The American Indian Religious Freedom Act: Ceremonies Were Illegal

**Traditional Native American religious practices were criminalized until August 11, 1978.** The American Indian Religious Freedom Act (AIRFA) was required because—despite the First Amendment—Native Americans were the only Americans whose religious practices needed special legislation.

**Before AIRFA (1978), the following were illegal or severely restricted:**

- Sweat lodge ceremonies
- Sun Dance ceremonies
- Potlatch ceremonies
- Vision quests
- Possession of sacred objects (eagle feathers, medicine bundles)
- Access to sacred sites on federal lands
- Singing traditional songs and prayers
- Practicing traditional medicine
- Gathering of sacred plants

**The 1994 Amendment** was required to explicitly legalize peyote in traditional religious ceremonies after the Supreme Court failed to protect sacramental practices.

**For context**: The Midewiwin ceremonies that Nbiish's ancestors practiced on High Island could have resulted in arrest just **~47 years ago**. #### Birthright Citizenship Under Attack

In January 2025, Executive Order 14156 and the Birthright Citizenship Act of 2025 (S. 304/H.R. 569) targeted birthright citizenship. While these measures do not directly target Native Americans, they:

- **Reference Native American exclusion history** to reinterpret the Fourteenth Amendment
- Use *Elk v. Wilkins* (1884) as precedent—the very case that excluded Native Americans from citizenship
- Create a legal environment where citizenship itself is weaponized
- Federal judges temporarily blocked the order (March 2025), but appeals continue

**The message is clear**: Citizenship protections remain fragile. Rights granted can be contested, reinterpreted, or ignored by enforcement agencies.

### Treaty Rights Under Continuous Attack

Great Lakes Anishinaabe treaty rights (1836, 1837, 1842) are **legally affirmed** but face ongoing challenges:

- **Walleye Wars (1980s-1990s)**: Violence against treaty fishermen exercising legal rights
- **State enforcement conflicts**: Attempts to require state licensing despite treaty protections
- **Resource pressures**: Commercial fishing decline, ecological challenges
- **Mille Lacs Band v. Minnesota (1999)**: Supreme Court upheld 1837 treaty rights, but only 5-4

**The lesson**: Legal victories do not guarantee safety. Rights on paper can still be contested, ignored, or undermined in practice.
